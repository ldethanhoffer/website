pseudoinverses..threefold

2017-05-20 · 818 words · 4 minute read
linear regression · linear algebra

It is a curiousity of linear algebra that standard textbooks rarely discuss pseudoinverses. It never really made sense to me as they really do answer a very natural question on linear transformations in a very elegant and succinct way.

For our purposes, they also play a crucial role in machine learning as they can be used to express the the normal equation, whose roots are the solution to the problem of linear regression.

In this post, we’ll take a look at pseudoinverses from three slightly different perspectives. We’ll begin by asking the following (admittedly opaque) question:

Let $L:V\longrightarrow W$ be any linear map between vector spaces. Is it possible to define a notion of (unique) “inverse”?

Of course, we will have to broaden the notion of inverse if the map is not bijective, since one of two problems could arise: $L$ could have a nonzero kernel or the image of $L$ could be a strictly smaller subspace of $W$. Luckily, vector spaces have a neat property which allow us to do a little more if one of these two situations occur:

Let $U\subset V$ be a subspace. Then $U$ has a (non-unique) complementary subspace $U'$ satisfying $U\oplus U'=V$

We can thus write the linear map $L$ as

$$L: \ker (L)\oplus U_V\longrightarrow \textrm{im} (L)\oplus U_W $$

Using this decomposition, we can assign a sort of “pre-image” $v \in V$ for any vector $w \in W$. Indeed, if we just consider the component $w’=\pi (w)$ of the summand $\textrm{im}(L)$, there exist some vector $v \in V$ such that $L(V)=w’$ by definition. Note that this $v$ is not unique, but it is not hard to see that the component $u=\pi_{U_V}(v)$ in the summand $U_V$ is independent of the choice of $v$. This leads to the following definition:

A pseudo-inverse for $L$ is a linear map $L^+:W\longrightarrow V$ defined by $$L^+(w)=u \iff L(v)=\pi_{\textrm{im}(L)}(w) \textrm{ for some } v\in V \textrm{ and } \pi_{U_V}(v)=u$$ where $\ker (L)\oplus U_V=V$ and $\textrm{im} (L)\oplus U_W =W$

We could thus intuitively summarize pseudo-inverses as follows:

a pseudo-inverse assigns to the right component of a vector $w \in W$ the right component of a pre-image $v \in V$

Another way of viewing pseudo-inverses starts with the following straightforward observation:

The restriction of $L$ to $L_{U_V}:U_V \longrightarrow \textrm{im} (L)$ is invertible
Let's denote the inverse of this restriction as $L^+_{\text{im}(L)}:\textrm{im} (L)\longrightarrow U_V$. For our second definition, we now wish to naturally extend the map $L^+$ to the whole of $W$. To do this, we simply need to choose a natural canididate for a map $L^+_{U_W}:U_W\longrightarrow \ker(L) $. Now, note that $L_{\ker(L)}:\ker(L)\longrightarrow U_W$ is the $0$ map. and so we'll define $L^+$ as the $0$ map as well. We now have:
The following are equivalent:
  1. $L^+$ is a pseudo-inverse
  2. $L_{U_W}^+$ is $0$ on $U_W$ and $L^+_{\text{im}(L)}$ is the inverse of $L$ on $\textrm{im}(L)$
Our next one-sentence description of pseudo-inverses is thus:
A pseudo-inverse $L$ coincides with the actual inverse of $L$ on the right components of $V$ and $W$ and $0$ on the other components.
The final description is the one most commonly found in the literature: Indeed, we could restate the second condition as $(L\circ L^+)_{U_V}=(L^+\circ L)_{\text{im}(L)}=\text{Id}$. Now, it's clear that the image of $L^+$ is precisely $U_V$, leading to the final characterization of a pseudo-inverse which arguably resembles the definition of an inverse the most:
The following are equivalent:
  1. $L^+$ is a pseudo-inverse
  2. $L_{U_W}^+$ is $0$ on $U_W$ and $L^+_{\text{im}(L)}$ is the inverse of $L$ on $\textrm{im}(L)$
  3. $(L\circ L^+)_{\text{im}{(L^+)}}=(L^+\circ L)_{\text{im}(L)}=\text{Id}$
Now, it follows from the second description that a pseudo-inverse is not unique. In fact, it is only uniquely defined after we choose a complements for the subspaces $\ker(L)\subset V$ and $\textrm{im} (L)\subset W$.
In other words, we have a map $$I: \{ (U_V,U_W )\vert \ker(L)\oplus U_V=V \textrm{ and }\textrm{im}(L)\oplus U_W=W\}\longrightarrow \textrm{Hom}(W,V)$$ The previous discussion shows that the image of $I$ is exactly the set of pseudo-inverses for $L$. As a fun exercise we invite the reader to prove the following claim: It turns out that
The map $I$ is injective, hence defines a 1-1 correspondence with pseudo-inverses.

If we now assume that $V$ and $W$ are finite dimensional inner product spaces, there is a canonical choice of complement for any subspace, (you guessed it): the orthogonal complement. The pseudo-inverse which corresponds to the choice $\big(\ker (L)^\perp,\text{im}(L)^\perp\big)$ is called the Moore-Penrose inverse. There is again a rather satisfying characterization for this specific choice of pseudo-inverse:

The following are equivalent:
  1. $L^+$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse to $L$
  2. $L^+$ is a pseudoinverse such that $L\circ L^+$ and $L^+\circ L$ are self-adjoint

I hope that gives a litle more insight into why Wikipedia insists on defining Moore-Penrose inverse through four rules the way it does: pseudo-inverses are a collection of maps that really do capture the notion of an inverse in a certain sense of the word, and the Moore-Penrose one really corresponds to a canonical choice of such a pseudo-inverse!
