the normal equation

2017-05-20 · 328 words · 2 minute read
linear regression · linear algebra

In this post we will discuss the so-called least squares problem (which is a horribly outdated name): which consists of finding $L:V\longrightarrow W$ be a linear map between two finite-dimensional normed spaces. Let $y \in W$. The problem asks to find an $x \in V$ such that $L(x)$ is as close to $y$ as possible, i.e. find $$\min_{x \in V} \vert \vert y-L(x)\vert\vert$$

We’ll do this in a few steps. First we describe condition of minimizing $\vert \vert y-L(x)\vert\vert$ geometrically. To this end, we recall the following lemma:

Let $U\subset V$ be a subspace and write $V=U\oplus U^\perp$. Let $pi_U$ be the projection. Then $pu_U(y)$ is the unique vector that minimizes the distance $\min_{u \in U}\vert \vert x-u\vert \vert$

It’s not that hard to see since for any other vector $u$ in the subspace $U$, we have: $$\vert \vert y-\pi_U(y)\vert \vert^2\le \vert \vert y-\pi_U(x)\vert \vert ^2+\vert \vert \pi_U(y)-u\vert \vert^2= \vert \vert y-u\vert \vert^2$$

By the Pythagorean theorem. The inequality becomes an equality precisely when $\vert \vert \pi_U(y)=u\vert \vert$, implying uniqueness.Applying this result to $U=\text{im}(L)$ yields

Let $y\in W$. The following are equivalent:
  1. $x$ minimizes the distance $\vert \vert y-L(x)\vert \vert$
  2. the vector $y-L(x)$ is perpendicular to the subspace $\text{im}(L)$
In other words the condition of $L(x)$ indeed minimizing the distance $\vert \vert y-L(x)\vert \vert$ has turned into $$0=\langle y-L(x),L(x')\rangle =\langle L^{\ast} y-L^\ast\circ L(x),x'\rangle \text{ for all } x' \in V $$ Which by nondegeneracy yields to the normal equation: $$(L^\ast\circ L)x =L^\ast y$$ We have actually already discussed the solution to this equation: indeed. let $L^+y$ denote the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse of $L$. Then $$(L^\ast \circ L)(L^+y)=L\ast \circ (L\circ L^+)y=L^*(L\circ L^+)^\ast y= (L L^+\circ L)^\ast y=L^\ast y $$ We ummarize the discussion in the following:
The following are equivalent:
  1. The distance $\vert \vert y-L(x)\vert \vert $ is minimal among $x \in V$
  2. $x$ is the projection of $y$ onto $\text{im}(L)$
  3. $x$ is the unique solution to the normal equation $L^*Lx=L^*y$
  4. $x=L^+y$, where $L^+$ is the Moore-Penrose inverse of $L$
